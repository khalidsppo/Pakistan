BRIEF HISTORY OF PROVICES:

PUNJAB:

Punjab is the most culturally and historically rich of Pakistan's four provinces. The Sutlej, Beas, Ravi, Chenab, and Jehlum rivers, which are tributaries of the Indus, are the sources of Punjab's name, which combines the words "Punj" (which means "five") and "Ab" (which means "water"). Half of the nation's population, or more than 70 million people, reside here. Geographically, it is a land of opposites, ranging from the sand dunes of Cholistan to the enormous, lush plains covered in greenery, from the unusual terrain of the Pothohar Plateau and Salt Range to the pine-covered foothills of the Himalaya. Punjab holds the distinction of having the nation's best-organized irrigation system. Here are cultivated the majority of Pakistan's agricultural products, such as sugarcane, cotton, wheat, rice, cotton, barley, maize, pulses, oilseed, and tobacco.

SINDH:

The historical homeland of the Sindhi people is Sindh, which is located in the southeast of the nation. The province of Sindh boasts Pakistan's second-largest economy, and Karachi, the provincial capital, is the country's biggest city and financial centre. Sindh is a place where time likes to rest its weary feet and where history lingers. This old land's past has been written and rewritten, leaving behind evident remnants that may be seen everywhere. It is the place that drew in the saints, the soldiers, and the generals from all over the world. Alexander the Great's invasion, the conquest of the Arabs, and battles with the Persian Empire are all recorded in history. It is the area where numerous migrating waterfowl reside.

KHAYBER PAKHTONKHAW (KPK):

One of Pakistan's four administrative provinces, Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, often known as "the land of the valiant Pakhtuns," is situated in the northwest of the country along the international border with Afghanistan. Formerly known as the North-West Frontier Province (NWFP), it underwent a name change in 2010 in response to a rising call from regional political leaders to reflect the province's predominant ethnic community (Pakhtuns). Also, there are lots of options for adventure and cultural tourism in this province.

BALOUCHISTAN:

The largest of Pakistan's four provinces, Balochistan, covers an area of 347,190 sq km, or 43.6 percent of the country's total landmass. In comparison to the other provinces, it has the smallest share and a clustered population. Balochistan is a vast plateau with difficult terrain that is split into basins by suitable height and roughness ranges. Balochistan's geographical region can be broadly split into the following four main zones: lowlands, deserts, and higher and lower highlands.

GILGIT BALTISTAN (GB):

One of Pakistan's top tourist destinations is Gilgit-Baltistan. Five of the "eight-thousanders" and more than fifty peaks higher than 7,000 metres are located in Gilgit-Baltistan (23,000 ft). The 72,496 sq km Gilgit Baltistan region is located in the far north of Pakistan and is home to almost 2 million people. On the north, it shares a border with China; on the east, with India; and on the west, with Afghanistan and the Central Asian nations. Famous worldwide for its breathtaking natural beauty is Gilgit Baltistan. The attractions include distinctive topographies, evergreen woods, chilly deserts, snow-capped mountains, and a rich cultural legacy. It is a well-known tourist destination and has long been a haven for those who love the mountains, hiking, and the great outdoors. There is consistently cool weather available.
